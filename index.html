TL;DR for Your Situation

You're targeting 50%+ net profit margins at a boutique consulting firm with $50K-$100K+ engagements.

  • Total talent spend should be 25-35% of revenue to hit 50% margins (assuming 10-15% overhead)
  • Partner compensation: 25-33% of revenue they generate is the sweet spot for boutiques at your margin target
  • This is achievable because your model is expert-led (trainers ARE the product) with minimal leverage/overhead
  • The "rule of thirds" (33/33/33) is the standard framework but most firms don't hit it — you're targeting something more aggressive and it's realistic for your model

Why Boutiques Can Run Leaner

  • No massive office overhead, recruiting infrastructure, or middle management layers
  • Principals deliver the work themselves (no leverage pyramid draining margin)
  • Premium pricing based on expertise, not headcount
  • Lower overhead (10-15% vs. 25-35% at large firms)

2. The "Rule of Thirds" Framework

The classic professional services model splits revenue into thirds:

CategoryTarget %
Compensation (all talent)~33%
Overhead (rent, tech, admin, marketing)~33%
Profit~33%

Reality check: Most firms don't actually achieve this. SPI Research's 2024 industry average EBITDA was only 9.8% — the worst in 5 years. The rule of thirds is aspirational for most; only top-quartile firms approach it.

The implied billing multiple: If you pay someone $150K, they need to generate ~$450K in revenue (3x multiple). This is the standard "3x rule" in consulting.

Sources: David Maister, "Managing the Professional Service Firm"; LeanLaw; SPI Research 2025.

3. Partner Compensation Models in Consulting

Eat What You Kill (EWYK)

  • Partner keeps a % of revenue they originate and/or deliver
  • Origination credits: 15-25% of collected fees
  • Production/delivery credits: 25-40%
+ Simple, high motivation - Can discourage collaboration

Lockstep

  • Compensation tied to seniority/tenure, not individual production
  • Encourages collaboration and knowledge-sharing
  • Used by McKinsey, many law firms
+ Fosters teamwork - Less common at small firms

Hybrid / Modified Lockstep (most common today)

  • 60-70% base (tied to role/seniority) + 30-40% variable (tied to performance)
  • Balances stability with incentive alignment
  • Typical at mid-size and boutique firms
+ Best of both worlds - More complex to administer

Finder-Minder-Grinder Split

  • Finder (brings in work): 15-25% credit
  • Minder (manages engagement): up to 20% credit
  • Grinder (does the work): 25-40% credit
  • One person can wear multiple hats (common in boutiques)

Sources: Ampleo 2026 compensation guide; PerformLaw originator/manager/worker analysis; Mosaic consulting profitability benchmarks.

Partner Compensation Framework

For a partner running a business line, here's what the data supports:

Option A: Revenue Share (EWYK-style)

  • 25-33% of revenue from their business line
  • If they generate $500K/year → $125K-$165K comp
  • If they generate $1M/year → $250K-$330K comp
+ Pure alignment, scales naturally - Volatile income for partner

Option B: Base + Variable

  • Base: $80K-$120K (provides stability)
  • Variable: 15-25% of revenue above a threshold
  • Total target: 25-35% of revenue generated
+ Partner has a floor - Firm bears downside risk

Option C: Profit Share

  • Partner gets 30-50% of profit (not revenue) from their line
  • If line does $800K revenue, $500K profit → partner gets $150K-$250K
+ Incentivizes efficiency - Requires transparent cost allocation
